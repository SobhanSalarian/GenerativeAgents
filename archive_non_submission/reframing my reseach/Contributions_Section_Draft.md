## 1.4 Contributions

This thesis reframes the validation of generative agent-based models (GABMs) from the question of *whether* individual agents appear believable to the question of *how* individual believability relates to valid emergent coordination — and how that relationship can be measured, characterised, and diagnosed. Existing work establishes that validation is the central open problem for generative social simulation (Larooij & Törnberg, 2025) and proposes conceptual workflows and quality criteria for approaching it (Adornetto et al., 2025; Sen et al., 2025). What has been missing is not another framework but an *operational and diagnostic* account: one that measures the micro–macro relationship rather than asserting it, locates the regime in which the two levels come apart, and identifies the concrete mechanism through which collective validity does or does not emerge. This thesis provides that account, and in doing so it repositions individual believability as a measurable *diagnostic signal* of valid coordination rather than a guarantee of it. Its central result is a measurable demonstration of the believability–validity gap; its most robust mechanistic result is that successful coordination, where it occurs, rests on a static shared norm rather than on continuous reasoning. The specific contributions are as follows.

**C1 — An operational, human-validated dual-level measurement instrument for GABM validity.** The thesis develops a reproducible operationalisation of micro- and macro-level validity that renders the micro–macro question empirically measurable. In contrast to existing validation *frameworks*, which remain largely conceptual or workflow-oriented (Adornetto et al., 2025; Sen et al., 2025), this contribution is a working instrument: four micro-level believability metrics (behavioural consistency, memory coherence, planning plausibility, response naturalness) and a suite of macro-level coordination metrics (coordination success, sustainability, inequality, convergence, influence-network structure, and failure traceability), combined with a cross-provider LLM-as-judge protocol (generator and judge drawn from different model families, following Zheng et al., 2023) and a reliability-gated, human-validated blend (inter-rater agreement assessed by Krippendorff's α, with gating below α = 0.67). The instrument's standing rests on demonstrated construct validity — rubric specification, judge–human agreement, and a sensitivity analysis of the composite weighting — rather than on assertion, and it is deployed throughout the thesis to generate the evidence for the contributions below.

**C2 — A measurable demonstration of the believability–validity gap (the central contribution).** The critical literature warns, qualitatively, that believable agents need not yield valid simulations (Larooij & Törnberg, 2025; Munker et al., 2026). This thesis renders that warning concrete and measurable. It establishes that high individual believability is *necessary but not sufficient* for collective validity, and it demonstrates the gap directly through worked cases already present in the data: most clearly the information-consensus baseline, in which individually believable, persona-consistent agents converge confidently on the *incorrect* option (a textbook information cascade; Bikhchandani et al., 1992), and commons-dilemma trials in which believable agents nonetheless drive the shared resource to collapse. The thesis introduces a signed believability–validity discrepancy *metric* to quantify the gap in these cases. The contribution is the *demonstration and measurement* of decoupling within this study, not a generalised, externally validated index; the limits of generalisation (notably the near-zero variance of the enriched information-consensus conditions) are disclosed explicitly.

**C3 — The architectural drivers of coordination, and a focal-point mechanism for the reflection effect.** The thesis establishes which cognitive capabilities underwrite coordination, and — its most robust mechanistic result — *how* coordination is sustained when it succeeds. Two findings are delivered. (i) **Planning is the strongest micro-level predictor of coordination.** Contrary to a memory-centred expectation for cooperation in LLM agent collectives (Hishiki et al., 2026), planning plausibility, not memory coherence, is the dominant micro-level predictor of coordination success (standardised β = 0.65, p = 0.001). This is reported as a robust observational finding that runs against the memory-centred expectation; it is *not* claimed as a causal or cognitive-primacy result, because planning plausibility sits closer to the action in the agents' serial cognitive pipeline and its predictive dominance may partly reflect that proximity (a confound a causal manipulation would have to break — see Future Work). (ii) **The reflection effect coincides with a static focal-point norm rather than continuous reasoning.** Adding scenario-level reflection produces a *categorical*, not incremental, improvement in coordination (coordination success 10% → 70%; mean sustainability 0.138 → 0.931). Direct inspection of the full condition shows that this gain coincides with reflection *saturation*: only three unique reflection strings are generated across all 100 steps, of which two account for 95.8% of all instances. The thesis interprets and evidences this as a focal-point mechanism — agents converge on a small, stable, shared coordination norm and repeat it — which provides a concrete, data-grounded account of how macro-level validity emerges in this architecture, and which reframes a phenomenon that might otherwise be dismissed as a limitation into an explanatory result.

**C4 — Trace-based early-warning diagnostics and a failure-trace taxonomy (exploratory).** The thesis contributes (i) a leakage-safe early-warning analysis that asks whether coordination collapse can be forecast from the opening steps of a simulation's micro-level traces — trained and evaluated with **trial-level** splits (never trace-step splits) and nested cross-validation, reported with lead time and precision–recall rather than accuracy alone — and (ii) a restrained, reproducible failure-trace taxonomy that links recurring micro-level believability failures to macro-level coordination breakdown. This adapts predictive process monitoring from business-process event logs (Galanti et al.) to generative social simulation, and complements general multi-agent failure taxonomies (Cemri et al.) with one grounded specifically in the micro–macro coordination relationship. Because composite believability is strongly associated with architectural condition, this contribution is framed as an **exploratory proof of concept**: it is explicitly tested for whether it forecasts collapse *within* an architectural condition rather than merely recovering condition identity, and is reported as predictive evidence only to the extent that it does.

Collectively, these contributions move the validation of GABMs from the *assertion* of a micro–macro relationship to its measurement and to the demonstration of where, and why, it breaks down: a working instrument (C1); a measurable demonstration of the believability–validity gap (C2); the architectural drivers of coordination together with a focal-point mechanism for the principal architectural effect (C3); and exploratory trace-based diagnostics for anticipating failure (C4). Throughout, individual believability is treated as a *diagnostic signal* of valid coordination rather than a proven cause: it tracks, thresholds, and (provisionally) forecasts collective validity, and the two demonstrably decouple. The empirical claims are established within a single, well-characterised agent architecture (Park et al., 2023) and two structurally distinct coordination tasks; the instrument and designs are architecture-agnostic by construction.

### Planned confirmatory experiments (future work, not claimed as results)

The following experiments are *designed* and reported strictly in the future tense; their outcomes are not pre-supposed and must not appear in the abstract or the contribution list until executed.

- **P1 — Reflection content-vs-process control (priority; low cost).** To confirm the focal-point interpretation in C3(ii), the two saturated reflection strings from the full condition will be injected statically into the matched memory-plus-planning architecture, against a tone-, length-, and position-matched placebo string. A substantial reproduction of the coordination gain would confirm that the benefit lies in the reusable *informational content* of reflection rather than in the *dynamic process* of generating it. Because the gain is already known to be carried by only three strings (two dominant), this experiment is inexpensive and serves primarily as a controlled confirmation of an in-hand observation.
- **P2 — Planning vs memory manipulation (optional; confound disclosed).** A manipulation that independently degrades planning content and memory content could in principle test the C3(i) finding causally. However, in the agents' serial pipeline memory feeds planning, so such a manipulation is confounded by pipeline proximity and cannot, on its own, establish cognitive primacy. It is retained only as an optional robustness ablation with this confound disclosed, not as a causal test.
- **P3 — Believability × information-accuracy factorial (further future work).** A fully interventional factorial is uninformative in the commons dilemma, where surface believability has no inter-agent channel through which to act, and only marginally informative in the present information-consensus task, where the enriched conditions show near-zero variance. It is reserved for a future communication-bearing task with restored variance.
