# Diagnosing the Believability–Validity Gap in LLM Multi-Agent Coordination

## ABSTRACT (replacement)

Generative agents powered by large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as autonomous actors in agent-based models (ABMs) to simulate social and organisational behaviour. Although such agents readily produce fluent conversations, memories, and plans, a central methodological problem remains unresolved: an agent that appears believable at the individual level need not produce valid behaviour at the group level. Existing work recognises this micro–macro problem but addresses it largely through conceptual validation frameworks and quality criteria, while the evidence that individual believability relates to collective outcomes remains observational.

This thesis moves the validation of generative agent-based models (GABMs) from assertion towards operational measurement, characterisation, and diagnosis. It develops a reproducible, human-validated dual-level instrument that scores individual believability (behavioural consistency, memory coherence, planning plausibility, response naturalness) and emergent coordination (coordination success, sustainability, inequality, convergence, influence structure, and failure traceability). Using a four-condition architectural ablation — baseline; memory; memory plus planning; and full memory-planning-reflection — across two structurally distinct coordination tasks, a commons dilemma and an information-consensus task (120 trials in total), it characterises the believability–coordination relationship and, above all, the regime in which the two levels decouple.

The central finding is a measurable **believability–validity gap**: high individual believability is necessary but not sufficient for valid collective behaviour. In the information-consensus task, individually believable, persona-consistent agents converge confidently on an *incorrect* option, reproducing a classic information cascade in which high individual plausibility coincides with collective invalidity. Around this result the thesis establishes that composite believability is positively associated with coordination and exhibits an observed lower region below which coordination effectively never succeeds; that planning plausibility — not memory coherence — is the strongest individual-level predictor of coordination, contrary to a memory-centred expectation; and that the addition of reflection produces a categorical rather than incremental improvement. Crucially, the thesis traces *how* this categorical gain arises: it coincides with reflection saturation — only three unique reflection strings are generated across the full condition, of which two account for 95.8% of all instances — indicating that successful coordination rests on a static, shared coordination norm acting as a focal point, rather than on continuous dynamic reasoning.

On this basis the thesis contributes a signed believability–validity discrepancy metric that renders the qualitative "believable-but-invalid" warning measurable within this study, and an exploratory leakage-safe trace-based early-warning analysis that asks whether coordination collapse can be forecast from the opening steps of a simulation's micro-level traces, together with a reproducible failure-trace taxonomy. A low-cost confirmatory experiment is designed and reported as planned work: a static injection of the saturated reflection content against a matched placebo, to confirm that the categorical benefit lies in the reusable informational *content* of reflection rather than in the *process* of generating it. Together these contributions reposition individual believability as a measurable diagnostic signal of valid coordination — necessary but not sufficient — and provide a rigorous, reusable basis for judging when generative agents are trustworthy components of social simulation.

## INTRODUCTION — opening (replacement for the first four paragraphs)

Agent-based modelling (ABM) studies complex systems in which large-scale patterns emerge from interactions among many individual agents. Traditionally, each agent's behaviour is specified through explicit rules, equations, or decision heuristics. Large language models (LLMs) have changed this design space: instead of relying only on hand-coded rules, researchers can now build *generative agents* that reason in natural language, maintain memories, form plans, and interact socially within simulated environments (Park et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Ghaffarzadegan et al., 2024). This makes generative agents attractive for modelling social, organisational, and economic systems in which human-like interpretation and communication matter.

This capability also introduces a validation problem. A generative simulation can appear convincing simply because its agents produce fluent explanations and contextually appropriate actions — yet surface plausibility does not establish that the simulation is valid as an ABM. Validity in the ABM tradition requires adequacy at two levels: the *micro* level, concerning the plausibility of individual behaviour, and the *macro* level, concerning whether the system produces emergent patterns that are consistent, meaningful, and explainable (Windrum et al., 2007; Moss & Edmonds, 2005). The open question for generative ABMs (GABMs) is whether these two levels are in fact connected: does individual believability correspond to valid collective behaviour, or can the two come apart?

Recent work has begun to engage this question, but in two limited ways. LLM-agent evaluation largely assesses agents in isolation or on task benchmarks (Liu et al., 2024; Mohammadi et al., 2025), while emerging GABM-validation work proposes conceptual workflows and quality criteria for the micro–macro problem (Adornetto et al., 2025; Sen et al., 2025; Larooij & Törnberg, 2025). What is missing is not another framework but an *operational and diagnostic* account: one that measures the micro–macro relationship rather than asserting it, identifies *where* the two levels decouple, and explains the mechanism by which collective validity emerges when it does. Importantly, where a relationship between individual plausibility and collective outcomes has been reported, the evidence is observational, and enriching an agent's cognitive architecture tends to raise believability and coordination together. This thesis therefore does not claim that believability *causes* valid coordination in general. It advances the more defensible and, for practitioners, more useful position that individual believability is a measurable *diagnostic signal* of valid collective behaviour — an early indicator that can be tracked and, crucially, can fail — rather than a guarantee of it.

This thesis addresses that gap. It builds an operational, human-validated dual-level instrument and uses it to *measure, characterise, and diagnose* the relationship between individual believability and emergent coordination. Generative agents are placed in two structurally distinct coordination tasks — a commons dilemma and an information-consensus task — under a four-condition architectural ablation (baseline; memory; memory plus planning; full memory-planning-reflection). The thesis establishes the form of the relationship and its threshold; demonstrates and quantifies a clear regime of micro–macro decoupling, in which individually believable agents produce an invalid collective outcome — the central result; shows that planning, not memory, is the strongest individual-level predictor of coordination, contrary to a memory-centred expectation; and explains the categorical improvement from reflection as the emergence of a static, shared focal-point norm rather than continuous reasoning. It then renders the relationship usable through a believability–validity discrepancy metric and an exploratory trace-based early-warning analysis of coordination collapse, and designs a low-cost confirmatory experiment to verify the focal-point account of the reflection effect.

## AIMS, RESEARCH QUESTIONS & HYPOTHESES (replacement for Section 3)

### Aim

The aim of this thesis is to **measure, characterise, and diagnose** the relationship between individual-level believability and emergent group-level coordination in generative agent-based models — in particular, to render the *believability–validity gap* measurable, and to explain the mechanism by which collective validity emerges — delivering a reusable, human-validated instrument and trace-based diagnostics. This refines into five objectives:

1. To operationalise micro- and macro-level validity for GABMs as a working, human-validated measurement instrument (rather than a conceptual framework).
2. To characterise, across two coordination tasks and four architectural conditions, the form of the relationship between individual believability and emergent coordination, including any threshold.
3. To demonstrate and quantify the regime in which individual believability and collective validity *decouple*.
4. To determine which cognitive capabilities — memory, planning, reflection — are most associated with coordination, and to explain the mechanism of the principal architectural effect.
5. To develop diagnostic tools that detect, and provisionally forecast, coordination failure from micro-level believability traces.

### Research questions

- **RQ1 (measurement).** What micro- and macro-level criteria validly and reliably operationalise individual believability and emergent coordination in GABMs?
- **RQ2 (decoupling — central).** Under what conditions do individual believability and collective validity *decouple*, and can the gap between them be quantified into a usable diagnostic?
- **RQ3 (association and threshold).** To what extent, and in what functional form, is individual believability associated with emergent coordination, and is there a believability region below which coordination effectively never succeeds?
- **RQ4 (drivers and mechanism).** Which cognitive capabilities — memory, planning, reflection — are most associated with coordination, and by what mechanism does the categorical reflection effect operate: continuous reasoning, or convergence on a static shared norm?
- **RQ5 (prediction and diagnosis).** Can coordination collapse be predicted from the early steps of a simulation's micro-level believability traces, beyond merely recovering the architectural condition, and what recurring failure types link micro-level breakdown to macro-level collapse?

### Hypotheses

**Group 1 — established findings on the existing 120-trial dataset.**

- **H1.** Higher composite believability is positively associated with stronger group coordination. *(Supported: ρ = 0.55, commons dilemma; ρ = 0.75, information consensus.)*
- **H2.** The relationship is non-linear, with a believability region below which coordination effectively never succeeds. *(Supported: observed lower boundary ≈ 0.36–0.39; reported as a within-study region, not a universal threshold.)*
- **H3.** Conditions with richer cognitive architecture exhibit higher believability than weaker conditions. *(Partially supported: monotone overall, but the memory-plus-planning and full conditions are not separable on believability alone.)*
- **H4.** Among the micro-level capabilities, *planning plausibility* — not memory coherence — is the strongest predictor of coordination. *(Supported: standardised β = 0.65, p = 0.001; against the memory-centred expectation of Hishiki et al., 2026. Reported as an observational predictor, not a cognitive-primacy claim: planning plausibility sits closer to the action in the serial pipeline, so its dominance may partly reflect pipeline proximity. The original pre-registered H4₀ — that memory coherence and behavioural consistency would dominate — is not supported.)*
- **H5 (decoupling — central).** Individual believability and collective validity decouple in identifiable regimes — most clearly where individually believable agents nonetheless produce collective failure — quantifiable by a signed believability–validity discrepancy metric. *(Supported: information-consensus baseline, where believable agents converge on the incorrect option, and commons-dilemma trials where believable agents drive the resource to collapse.)*
- **H6 (reflection mechanism — focal-point norm).** The categorical coordination gain from reflection is sustained by a static, shared coordination norm rather than by continuous dynamic reasoning. *(Supported by direct evidence of reflection saturation: only three unique reflection strings are generated across the full condition's 100 steps, of which two account for 95.8% of all instances, yet the gain is categorical — consistent with convergence on a focal point.)*

**Group 2 — diagnostic re-analysis (existing data).**

- **H7 (early warning).** Coordination collapse is predictable from the first K steps of a trial's micro-level believability traces, beyond what is explained by architectural condition alone. *(Evaluated by leakage-safe re-analysis with trial-level cross-validation and an explicit within-condition test; reported with lead time and precision–recall, and as exploratory where the within-condition signal is weak.)*

**Group 3 — planned confirmatory experiment (future tense; outcome not pre-supposed).**

- **H8 (reflection content vs process — planned).** The reflection gain is driven by the reusable *informational content* of reflection, not the *process* of generating it. This will be tested by statically injecting the two saturated reflection strings into the matched memory-plus-planning architecture against a tone-, length-, and position-matched placebo; a substantial reproduction of the gain would confirm the content (focal-point) account established observationally in H6.
